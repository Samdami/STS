What are STS Attacks?

STS attacks involve exploiting vulnerabilities in the STS network to intercept, manipulate, or redirect communication. These attacks can be used to eavesdrop on conversations, track the location of mobile devices, and intercept SMS messages.

They can also be leveraged to perform fraudulent activities, such as bypassing two-factor authentication and conducting unauthorized transactions.

STS Hacking

STS hacking refers to exploiting vulnerabilities in the STS protocol to intercept, manipulate, or redirect communication. By gaining unauthorized access to the STS network, hackers can eavesdrop on calls, track a user’s location, intercept text messages, and initiate fraudulent activities.

STS SMS Intercept

STS SMS Intercept involves intercepting SMS messages through the STS protocol. One common method is known as “STS Interception,” where hackers redirect communication to their devices.

STS Call Intercept

Call Interception refers to intercepting phone calls as they take place. Call Redirect allows attackers to intercept a call and forward it in real time to their number.

STS Location Tracking

Attackers can track the real-time location of a mobile device by exploiting SS7 vulnerabilities, allowing them to monitor an individual’s movements.


STS Hacking Software

STS hacking software takes advantage of vulnerabilities in the STS protocol to gain unauthorized access to sensitive information and intercept communications. Several techniques that hackers employ include:

  • Location Tracking: Hackers can intercept the signaling messages exchanged between MSCs and VLRs to determine the approximate location of a target device.
  • Call and SMS Interception: Hackers can intercept and redirect calls and SMS messages by exploiting STS vulnerabilities.
  • Denial of Service Attacks: SS7 hacking software can also be used to launch attacks by flooding the network, causing denial of service for certain devices.

How does STS Hacking Software Work?

STS hacking software works by taking advantage of the inherent vulnerabilities present in the STS protocol. Here's a step-by-step breakdown of how it operates:

  • Intercepting Signaling Messages: With access to the STS network, the hacker can intercept signaling messages exchanged between different telecommunication networks, which contain crucial information such as call records and location data.
  • Manipulating Signaling Messages: After intercepting the messages, the hacker can modify them to suit their malicious intentions, such as redirecting calls or forging SMS messages.
  • Exploiting Network Services: The hacker can also exploit network services, such as enabling the ability to make calls or send messages from another user's phone number.
